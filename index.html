SOFT STEPS

Team: Jessica Ocampo, Cici Chen, Sophia Ho, Joy Li, Estella Langenhoven
Duration: 2 Months


Role:
GAME MECHANICS DESIGNER UX/UI DESIGNER PS.P5 DEVELOPER

Tools:
#JS.P5 #VSCODE #PROCREATE


OVERVIEW

Soft Steps is a 2D pixel platformer game designed to raise awareness about hyperalgesia by simulating sensory overload through gameplay. Players navigate soft, rough, and sharp surfaces while managing a sensitivity meter, collecting healing orbs, and progressing through gardens, caves, and forests. The art direction emphasizes cozy, muted aesthetics to evoke calm, contrasting typical bright platformer visuals.


PROBLEM

The game was created to simulate the daily sensory challenges faced by individuals with hyperalgesia. Popular platformers often prioritize excitement and difficulty but rarely consider how overstimulation can affect playability. Our goal was to introduce an empathetic lens into game design, balancing challenge with accessibility.


PROCESS

    Research: Our team investigated the lived experience of hyperesthesia and hyperalgesia, exploring symptoms like pain sensitivity and how textures can become overwhelming. Research influenced how we designed surface types‚Äîsoft, rough, and sharp‚Äîand informed gameplay mechanics like the sensitivity bar. We also looked into global accessibility design principles to ensure inclusivity‚Äã.

    Ideation: We brainstormed around metaphors and mechanics that could convey discomfort without being punitive. The narrative of searching for a lost cat served as a grounding emotional anchor. Multiple design sketches and platforming tests helped visualize the game flow before coding began.



    This is a screenshot from our low-fidelity.

    Design: The pixelated design direction was chosen to enhance clarity and mood while maintaining simplicity. Levels include floral gardens, forest paths, and caves, each with soft, muted tones. Custom assets, AI-assisted visuals, and consistent UI elements made the experience more cohesive‚Äã.

    Testing: Playtesting was conducted with classmates and professors across both development phases. Feedback focused on platform difficulty, jump range accuracy, and visual readability. Based on this, we adjusted platform spacing, added checkpoints, and ensured all jumps were always reachable‚Äã.




This is a screenshot from level one of Soft Steps.

SOLUTION

The final game immerses players in a soothing but challenging world where overstimulation is a core mechanic. The inclusion of healing orbs, checkpoints, and a sensitivity bar allows players to self-regulate their experience. These features collectively simulate the heightened awareness experienced with hyperalgesia.


IMPACT

    Results: By translating pain sensitivity into interactive mechanics, Soft Steps promotes empathy through play. The project fostered discussion around invisible disabilities in digital spaces and how gameplay can be a vehicle for storytelling and understanding. It also encouraged more inclusive design thinking within our peer group.

    What was the outcome? The final product was a four-level platformer complete with boss mechanics, checkpoints, collectibles, and a pause menu. All members contributed equally to visuals, coding, testing, and polish. The game was successfully submitted and received positive feedback for its theme cohesion and originality.

    Measurable metrics: Key success metrics included smoother player navigation, reduced sensitivity -related frustration, and more consistent feedback in class reviews. In our playtests, 80% of testers reached the end goal without overloading their sensitivity meter after the final tweaks‚Äã.

WHAT DID I LEARN?

    1. I learned how to translate abstract physical sensations into game logic and visual storytelling.
    2. This project pushed me to code more effectively, troubleshoot level balancing, and work fluidly across team roles.
    3. It also reinforced how feedback and iteration are key to accessible design.


Challenges & how I overcame them:

    One major challenge was ensuring all platforms remained jumpable across levels with different heights. We resolved this by implementing a grid-based jump validation system.

    Another challenge was aligning visual tone with gameplay pacing‚Äîwe achieved this through custom assets and revised level timing‚Äã.

Skills Gained:

    I gained hands-on experience in game development using p5.js, visual design for accessibility, and user-centered testing. I also strengthened my skills in team communication, code optimization, and adaptive design based on stakeholder feedback.

MAPLE LEAF FOOD'S INSPECTION FORM

Team: Jessica Ocampo & Jenani Yoganathan
Duration: 3 Months


Role:
UX RESEARCHER UX DESIGNER APP DEVELOPER

Tools:
#FIGMA #POWER APPS #CANVA


OVERVIEW

The project involved designing a tablet application prototype as part of Maple Leaf Foods‚Äô "Path to Paperless" initiative. This application served as a universal form prototype/design system for employees to use at the plants, replacing paper forms such as inspection forms. It was developed exclusively for Maple Leaf Foods employees, specifically for plant workers. The goal was to reduce paper usage across Maple Leaf Foods by providing a digital solution that streamlined form-based tasks in an accessible and efficient way.


PROBLEM

Plant workers relied on paper-based forms, which were inefficient, environmentally unfriendly, and challenging to manage. Maple Leaf Foods wanted to digitize this process to align with their sustainability goals. Employees wearing gloves needed the application to be functional despite limited tactile precision. Employees who were less tech-savvy needed a user-friendly interface. The forms needed to balance simplicity and visual appeal while maintaining functionality. The paper-based process was resource-intensive, less environmentally sustainable, and prone to inefficiencies. Digitization offered a way to enhance operational effectiveness and align with Maple Leaf Foods‚Äô sustainability goals.


PROCESS

    Research: Conducted interviews and user testing with plant employees to understand their needs, workflows, and challenges with transitioning to digital forms.

    Ideation: Brainstormed solutions collaboratively using Figma to create low-fidelity wireframes, which evolved into high-fidelity prototypes. Canva was used to source icons to enhance the visual design.


    This is the original design which got changed due to a new criteria to have no MLF branding.

    Design: Focused on creating simple yet visually appealing forms with key features such as dark mode and light mode. The high-fidelity designs were built in Figma.

    Testing: Validated designs through usability testing with employees to ensure they were intuitive and functional, even for users with gloves or limited tech experience. Prototypes were then built in Microsoft Power Apps for further testing.


This is the final product.

SOLUTION

A simple, user-friendly interface. Support for dark mode and light mode to cater to user preferences and visual comfort. Compatibility with glove use to accommodate plant workers. Icons and visual elements to enhance clarity and usability. The design addressed user needs by being intuitive and accessible for a wide range of employees, reducing the learning curve for older or less tech-savvy workers. It eliminated paper dependency, aligning with the company‚Äôs sustainability objectives.


IMPACT

    Results: The prototype was handed off to the development team for implementation. It set the foundation for a scalable, reusable design system for other form-based applications across Maple Leaf Foods.

    What was the outcome? Positioned the company to significantly reduce paper usage at plants, contributing to their "Path to Paperless" initiative.

    Measurable metrics: While specific metrics were not mentioned, the project advanced Maple Leaf Foods‚Äô sustainability and digital transformation goals.

WHAT DID I LEARN?

    1. How to work cross-functionally with various teams and stakeholders.
    2. Best practices for designing tablet applications, especially for industrial use cases.
    3. The importance of user-centric design in making technology accessible.


Challenges & how I overcame them:

    One challenge involved designing for users who wear gloves. The solution was to simplify touchpoints and conduct usability testing while users were wearing gloves.

    Another challenge was ensuring the application appealed to older, less tech-savvy employees. To address this, simplicity was prioritized, and the application was extensively tested with this user group.

Skills Gained:

    Developed proficiency in Figma for design and prototyping, gained experience with Microsoft Power Apps for low-fidelity interactive prototypes, and enhanced understanding of design standards for iPad/tablet applications.

CROSSCULTURA

Team: Jessica Ocampo, Isabelle Cho, Jackie Zhang
Duration: 2 Months


Role:
UX RESEARCHER UX DESIGNER STYLY DEVELOPER

Tools:
#FIGMA #STYLY #PREMIERE PRO

SHORT VIDEO

OVERVIEW

The project focused on developing a Cultural Adjustment and Language Learning App to support international students in overcoming language barriers, cultural adjustment challenges, and feelings of isolation. The app was designed for international undergraduate students as the primary target audience, addressing their unique challenges.

The goal was to create an augmented reality app that helps international students improve language skills, understand cultural nuances, and practice real-life scenarios. The app aimed to alleviate anxiety and mental health challenges, fostering better academic and social integration.


PROBLEM

International students often struggle with language barriers, adapting to cultural norms and feelings of isolation, which intensify stress and mental health challenges. These difficulties make it harder for students to practice academic and conversational language, navigate cultural etiquette, and feel supported in their new environments. As a result, their academic performance, well-being, and ability to integrate into their communities suffer, highlighting a critical need for tailored mental health support and adjustment tools to address these unique challenges.


PROCESS

    Research: Methods Used: Think-aloud protocol, System Usability Scale (SUS) surveys, semi-structured interviews, and user interaction analytics. Feedback emphasized accessibility, intuitive use, and engaging features.

    Ideation: Brainstormed features like immersive scenarios (e.g., grocery shopping, lectures), NPC interactions, and gamification elements (XP points, levels).


    This is the brand style guideline.

    Design: Tools: Figma and Styly for high-fidelity prototypes. Implemented Universal Design principles, ensuring accessibility (e.g., high-contrast modes, text-to-speech, and voice commands).

    Testing: Validation Methods: User testing provided qualitative and quantitative insights. Issues like navigation, clarity of interface elements, and the lack of interactivity were identified and iterated upon.


This is the iterated prototype.

SOLUTION

The solution featured immersive AR scenarios simulating real-life settings, accessibility enhancements like voice commands and text-to-speech, and gamification elements such as XP rewards and progress tracking. A feedback system provided real-time insights, helping users practice language, adapt to cultural norms, reduce anxiety, and foster a sense of achievement.


IMPACT

    Results: The app demonstrated improved usability, with SUS scores indicating it was user-friendly, achieving a mean ease-of-use score of 3.75. Users provided positive feedback, praising features such as transcripts, adjustable audio speeds, and XP rewards for supporting mental health and enhancing engagement. Additionally, the simulated environments helped reduce anxiety by making learning less intimidating.

    What was the outcome? Users appreciated the app‚Äôs immersive and gamified approach, encouraging frequent use. Participants suggested the potential for increased student confidence and improved academic/social adjustment.

    Measurable metrics: Average SUS score: 74 (indicative of good usability). Reduced perceived complexity: Low complexity score (mean 1.5).

WHAT DID I LEARN?

    The project highlighted the importance of user-centered design and the value of continuous iteration based on feedback. Incorporating accessibility and inclusivity was shown to enhance usability and increase user satisfaction. Additionally, user testing proved invaluable for uncovering hidden pain points and identifying areas for improvement.


Challenges & how I overcame them:

    One challenge was ensuring intuitive navigation and interactivity within the app. This was addressed by adding headers, improving button clarity, and enhancing navigation paths. Another challenge involved balancing immersion and simplicity, which was resolved by streamlining unnecessary features, such as removing confusing visualizers.

Skills Gained:

    The project enhanced proficiency in prototyping tools like Figma and Styly. It also developed skills in conducting user research and analyzing both qualitative and quantitative data. Additionally, it emphasized applying Universal Design principles to ensure accessibility for all users.


IMPROVED FIGMA PROTOTYPING USER RESEARCH DATA ENHANCED DESIGN PRINCIPLES

MOODLE

Team: Jessica Ocampo, Cindy Choung, Joy Li, Cici Chen, Sophia Ho, Collin Yeung
Duration: 6 hours


Role:
UX RESEARCHER UX DESIGNER GRAPHIC DESIGNER

Tools:
#FIGMA #PROCREATE #ILLUSTRATOR

OVERVIEW

The project was about designing a mobile application aimed at raising awareness and reducing the stigma around mental health for pre-teens. It featured educational and engaging components to help foster a better understanding of mental health in a fun, interactive way. The application was developed for the Sun Life UXperience Designathon. The goal was to create an engaging and educational tool to help pre-teens understand mental health better, raise awareness, and combat the stigma surrounding it.


PROBLEM

The key problem was the lack of accessible, engaging resources aimed at reducing the stigma around mental health for pre-teens. Mental health awareness is often overlooked at a younger age, especially for those in the pre-teen demographic. The primary challenge was that pre-teens often don‚Äôt have access to or understanding of mental health resources that are both appropriate and engaging for their age. This could lead to misunderstandings or stigma around mental health. For Sun Life and the UXperience Designathon, addressing mental health stigma in the younger demographic could help in shaping a more informed, compassionate generation and strengthen the community's well-being and engagement.


PROCESS

    Research: We began by researching existing mobile health applications to analyze trends, features, and targeting user demographics. This helped ensure the app‚Äôs feasibility, usability, and engagement potential.

    Ideation: We kicked off the design process with brainstorming sessions to generate ideas, followed by group discussions to refine the concept. This collaborative approach helped us focus on features that would resonate with the pre-teen demographic.

This is a drawing of our low-fidelity.

    Design: We used tools like Figma for creating the low-fidelity sketches and prototypes, and Procreate for visual design elements. Our graphic designers worked on the visual aesthetics, while researchers ensured the design remained cohesive and appropriate for the target audience.

    Testing: We took the opportunity to consult with mentors during the event, who provided valuable insights that helped us edit and improve our designs and concepts. This feedback was crucial in validating our design choices and enhancing the app‚Äôs overall appeal and usability.


This is our brainstorming & final prototype file.

SOLUTION

The design featured interactive educational content, an easy-to-navigate interface, and visuals tailored to pre-teens. We focused on creating a fun and informative experience. By providing a non-intimidating space to learn about mental health, the design directly addressed the stigma surrounding it, helping pre-teens understand the topic in an accessible way.


IMPACT

The project received positive feedback during the pitch, with judges praising the engaging and educational design. Although there were no measurable metrics due to the 6-hour time limit, the judges appreciated the balance of usability, aesthetics, and educational value. They also provided constructive feedback, which helped refine our concept. As our first designathon, there was room for improvement, and we took the insights to enhance our future design processes.

WHAT DID I LEARN?

    1. I learned how to rapidly collaborate with a team to ideate, design, and prototype an application under tight time constraints.

    2. It was also a valuable experience in understanding how to tailor solutions for specific user demographics.


Challenges & how I overcame them:

    The biggest challenge was working within the time limit. We overcame this by efficiently dividing tasks, with each team member focusing on their strengths‚Äîgraphic design, research, and prototyping‚Äîwhile ensuring cohesive collaboration.

Skills Gained:

    I gained experience in rapid prototyping with Figma, collaborative brainstorming, and research on health-related mobile apps. I also honed my skills in presenting and pitching concepts to judges.
